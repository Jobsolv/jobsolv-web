SQL and Database Query Questions</h2><p>SQL and database management are the foundation of a business intelligence analyst&#39;s technical skills. These questions test your ability to extract, manipulate, and analyze data directly from its source. Interviewers use them to measure your fluency in SQL, your understanding of relational database concepts like joins and aggregations, and your approach to writing clean, efficient code.</p><p>Proficiency here is a must have, as you will spend significant time retrieving and preparing data for dashboards and reports. Failing this section signals an inability to perform core job functions.</p><h3>Example Interview Questions</h3><ul><li><strong>Easy:</strong> &quot;Write a SQL query to find all customers from California who have made a purchase in the last 30 days.&quot;</li><li><strong>Medium:</strong> &quot;Using window functions, write a query to calculate the month over month revenue growth for each product category.&quot;</li><li><strong>Hard:</strong> &quot;You have a query that joins four large tables and is running slowly. Case Study and Problem-Solving Questions</h2><p>Beyond technical skills, companies want to see how you think. Statistical Analysis and Hypothesis Testing Questions</h2><p>Statistical analysis separates data reporting from true business intelligence. These questions evaluate your ability to go beyond presenting numbers and instead validate insights, quantify uncertainty, and make data driven recommendations with confidence. Interviewers want to see if you can design and interpret experiments like A/B tests and explain complex concepts in simple business terms.</p><p>A strong grasp of statistics is crucial for any BI analyst expected to influence strategy. An inability to discuss p values or experimental design suggests you can only describe <em>what</em> happened, not <em>why</em> it happened or what to do next.</p><h3>Example Interview Questions</h3><ul><li><strong>Easy:</strong> &quot;Can you explain the difference between correlation and causation? When explaining a concept like a p-value, avoid a dry definition. Instead, frame it in the context of business risk: &quot;A low p-value means we can be confident the change was due to our actions, not random chance, reducing the risk of making a bad decision.&quot;</p><blockquote><p><strong>Expert Tip:</strong> When discussing A/B testing, bring up practical business considerations.
